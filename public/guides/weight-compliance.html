Chapter 1: Why Weight Compliance Matters

If you only read one chapter, make it this one.

Every year in Australia, thousands of caravans hit the road overloaded. Most owners don't know it. They loaded up the van, hitched up, and drove off — trusting that everything would be fine.

For many, it is fine. Until it isn't.

An overloaded caravan doesn't announce itself with a warning light or a beeping alarm. It feels a bit sluggish, maybe sways a touch more in crosswinds, takes a little longer to stop. Easy to dismiss. Easy to ignore.

Until a truck passes you on the Pacific Highway and the sway doesn't settle. Until you brake hard on a downhill and the trailer pushes the back of your vehicle sideways. Until a tyre blows at 100 km/h because it's been carrying 300 kg more than it was designed for.

Weight compliance isn't bureaucratic red tape. It's the difference between arriving safely and not arriving at all.

The Four Consequences of Being Overweight

1. Safety

This is the big one. Everything else is secondary.

An overloaded caravan changes the physics of your entire rig:

  • Braking distance increases dramatically. Your brakes were designed for a specific maximum load. Exceed it, and stopping distances can increase by 20-40%. At highway speeds, those extra metres can be the difference between stopping safely and rear-ending the car in front.

  • Sway becomes unpredictable. A rear-heavy or overloaded caravan is inherently unstable. Passing trucks, crosswinds, or even a slight steering correction can trigger a sway that builds on itself. Once it starts, it can be almost impossible to control.

  • Tyre blowouts. Every tyre has a load rating. Exceed it consistently and the tyre overheats, the sidewall weakens, and eventually it lets go. A caravan tyre blowout at speed can rip out plumbing, gas lines, and bodywork — and send the van into oncoming traffic.

  • Rollover risk. An overloaded, top-heavy caravan has a higher centre of gravity. Combine that with sway, and the chance of rollover increases significantly — particularly on curves and in emergency manoeuvres.

  • Suspension failure. Overloading compresses springs and shocks beyond their design limits. The vehicle sits lower, reducing ground clearance and changing the geometry of the steering and braking systems. Leaf springs can crack. Air bags can burst.

KEY POINT: In NSW Police caravan weigh operations, close to 40% of caravans tested were found to be overloaded. This isn't a rare problem — it's an epidemic.

2. Legal

Victoria's system is particularly harsh — the more overweight you are, the higher the fine. Exceed your limit by more than 15% and you're looking at over $1,500. And that's just the infringement notice. Court-imposed penalties can be significantly higher.

But the fine is the least of your worries. Police can also:

  • Issue a defect notice requiring immediate repair
  • Ground your caravan on the spot — you cannot drive it until the weight is reduced
  • Impound your vehicle in severe cases

Being grounded on the side of a highway 200 km from home, unloading gear onto the roadside while trucks blast past at 110 km/h — it happens more often than you'd think.

3. Insurance

This is the consequence that most caravanners don't consider until it's too late.

Most insurance policies contain a clause that voids coverage if the vehicle or caravan was being operated outside its manufacturer's specifications at the time of an incident. Exceeding GVM, ATM, or GCM counts as operating outside specifications.

What this means in practice:

  • Your caravan rolls over and is written off. You claim on insurance. The assessor checks the compliance plate, weighs the remaining contents, and determines the van was overloaded. Claim denied.

  • You rear-end someone because your overloaded rig couldn't stop in time. Their insurer comes after you for damages. Your insurer declines to cover you because you were overweight. You're personally liable.

  • Your tow vehicle's transmission fails under the strain of an overloaded combination. You claim on your vehicle warranty. The manufacturer checks the compliance data and determines you exceeded the GCM. Warranty voided.

WARNING: Insurance companies are becoming increasingly sophisticated at investigating weight compliance after accidents. Some now request weighbridge records as part of the claims process. "I didn't know I was overweight" is not a defence.

4. Mechanical Damage

Even if you never have an accident, never get fined, and never make an insurance claim, overloading still costs you:

  • Transmission damage — Automatic transmissions are the first casualty. The torque converter and gearbox work harder, run hotter, and wear faster when towing beyond their rated limits. A transmission rebuild costs $4,000–$8,000.

  • Suspension wear — Springs sag, shocks blow, bushings deteriorate. Your vehicle's ride height drops, changing suspension geometry and accelerating wear on ball joints and tie rod ends.

  • Premature tyre failure — Overloaded tyres run hotter, wear unevenly, and fail sooner. Replacing a set of light truck tyres runs $1,200–$2,000.

  • Brake wear — Brakes work harder, overheat more frequently, and wear out faster. Warped rotors, glazed pads, and boiled brake fluid are common in overloaded rigs.

  • Fuel consumption — An overloaded rig uses 15-25% more fuel. Over a 5,000 km trip, that's hundreds of dollars in extra diesel.

The "She'll Be Right" Myth

Australians are famously laid-back. It's one of our best qualities — until it comes to towing safety.

"She'll be right" has no place in weight compliance. Here's why guessing doesn't work:

  • Water weighs more than you think. A full 200-litre water tank adds 200 kg. That's the weight of two and a half adults.

  • Accessories add up invisibly. A bull bar (35 kg), roof rack (25 kg), long-range fuel tank (30 kg empty, 80 kg full), canopy (60 kg), drawer system (40 kg) — that's 240 kg of accessories before you've loaded a single bag.

  • The caravan's tare weight is a fantasy. The tare weight on the compliance plate is what the caravan weighed when it rolled off the production line — with nothing in it. No gas bottles, no spare tyre, no toolbox, no after-market air conditioning, no awning upgrade. The real empty weight is typically 100-300 kg higher than the plate says.

  • You can't feel the difference. The human body is terrible at sensing weight. A caravan that's 500 kg overweight doesn't feel 500 kg different. It feels normal — until something goes wrong.

The only way to know your weight is to measure it. That's what this guide is about.

Quick Self-Assessment: Are You at Risk?

Answer yes or no to each question:

  • I have never weighed my caravan when fully loaded for a trip
  • I don't know my vehicle's GCM (Gross Combination Mass)
  • I carry full water tanks when driving between campsites
  • I've added aftermarket accessories to my tow vehicle (bull bar, canopy, roof rack, etc.)
  • My caravan has had accessories added since purchase (air con, solar, batteries, etc.)
  • I carry "just in case" items that I rarely use on trips
  • I've never checked if my tow vehicle's GVM is exceeded when loaded with passengers and gear
  • I don't know what my towball weight is

If you answered yes to three or more questions, you're at risk of being overweight — and you may not know it.

Keep reading. By the end of this guide, you'll have the knowledge and tools to check, fix, and maintain weight compliance on every trip.

Chapter 2: Understanding the Numbers

The six weight figures every caravanner must know — explained simply.

Weight compliance comes down to six numbers. Learn these, and you'll understand more about towing safety than 90% of caravanners on the road.

The problem is that these numbers are buried in jargon and acronyms. GVM, ATM, GTM, GCM, TBM — it reads like alphabet soup. But each one is simple once you know what it means and why it matters.

Let's break them down.

The Big Six

1. Tare Weight

What it is: The weight of the vehicle or caravan as it left the factory — empty, with nothing added.

Think of it as: The starting point. The minimum weight before you add anything.

Where to find it: On the compliance plate (a metal plate usually found on the driver's door pillar for vehicles, or the A-frame/drawbar for caravans).

Why it matters: Tare is the baseline you subtract from the GVM or ATM to calculate your payload — the amount you can actually load.

WARNING: For caravans, the tare weight on the compliance plate is almost never the real empty weight. Dealers often add accessories before sale (awnings, air conditioning, solar panels, batteries, toolboxes). These aren't reflected on the compliance plate. The real empty weight could be 100-300 kg higher than what the plate says. The only way to know your true empty weight is to weigh it.

2. GVM — Gross Vehicle Mass

What it is: The maximum total weight your vehicle is allowed to be, fully loaded. This includes the vehicle itself, all passengers, all cargo, fuel, water, accessories, and the towball weight from the caravan pushing down on the back.

Think of it as: Your vehicle's absolute weight limit. Go over this, and you're breaking the law.

Where to find it: On the vehicle's compliance plate (usually on the driver's door pillar or under the bonnet).

The formula:

Vehicle payload = GVM − Tare weight

EXAMPLE: Your Toyota HiLux SR5 has a GVM of 3,050 kg and a tare weight of 2,110 kg.

Payload = 3,050 − 2,110 = 940 kg

That 940 kg has to cover: passengers, fuel above the amount in the tare measurement, all gear in the vehicle, aftermarket accessories (bull bar, canopy, roof rack, drawer system), AND the towball weight from the caravan.

KEY POINT: The towball weight (typically 200-350 kg) counts towards the tow vehicle's GVM, not the caravan's. This is the number one thing people forget. If your towball weight is 300 kg, that comes straight off your 940 kg payload, leaving only 640 kg for everything else.

3. ATM — Aggregate Trailer Mass

What it is: The maximum total weight your caravan is allowed to be, fully loaded and sitting on its own wheels — as if it were standing alone, not hitched to anything.

Think of it as: Your caravan's absolute weight limit.

Where to find it: On the caravan's compliance plate (usually on the A-frame or drawbar near the coupling).

The formula:

Caravan payload = ATM − Tare weight

EXAMPLE: Your Jayco Journey has an ATM of 2,342 kg and a tare weight of 1,912 kg.

Payload = 2,342 − 1,912 = 430 kg

That 430 kg has to cover: water tanks (full 95L = 95 kg), gas bottles (2 × 8.5 kg = 17 kg), food, clothing, bedding, tools, outdoor furniture, bike rack, annex, and everything else you put in the van.

430 kg sounds like a lot until you start adding things up. It goes fast.

4. GTM — Gross Trailer Mass

What it is: The maximum weight allowed on the caravan's own axles when it's coupled to the tow vehicle. It's the ATM minus the towball weight.

Think of it as: How much weight the caravan's wheels and axles are actually carrying when hitched up and ready to go.

Where to find it: On the caravan's compliance plate (not all caravans show this — it's more common on newer vans).

Why it matters: GTM is often lower than ATM because when the van is coupled, some of its weight transfers to the tow vehicle through the towball. The caravan's axles, wheels, and tyres are rated for the GTM, not the ATM.

The relationship:

ATM = GTM + Towball weight

Most people can safely focus on ATM and GVM and not worry about GTM separately — if you're within ATM and your towball weight is in the right range (8-12% of ATM), your GTM will be fine.

5. GCM — Gross Combination Mass

What it is: The maximum total weight of the entire rig — tow vehicle AND caravan combined, fully loaded.

Think of it as: The overall speed limit for the whole combination. Even if the vehicle and caravan are each individually within their limits, you can still be over on GCM.

Where to find it: On the tow vehicle's compliance plate (not the caravan's — GCM is set by the vehicle manufacturer).

The formula:

Actual combination mass = Loaded vehicle weight + Loaded caravan weight

This must not exceed GCM.

KEY POINT: GCM is the most commonly exceeded weight limit — and the one most people don't even know about. We'll cover the "GCM trap" in detail in Chapter 3.

6. Towball Weight (TBM — Tow Ball Mass)

What it is: The downward force the caravan exerts on the tow vehicle's towball coupling. When the van is hitched, some of its weight pushes down on the back of the vehicle.

Think of it as: The handshake between the vehicle and the caravan. Too light and the van sways dangerously. Too heavy and the vehicle's rear sags, lifting the front wheels and reducing steering and braking grip.

The target range: 8-12% of the caravan's loaded weight (ATM).

EXAMPLE: If your loaded caravan weighs 2,200 kg:

Calculation Result
Minimum towball weight (8%) 2,200 × 8% = 176 kg
Maximum towball weight (12%) 2,200 × 12% = 264 kg
Sweet spot (10%) 220 kg

WARNING: Your tow vehicle also has a maximum towball weight rating (shown on the compliance plate or in the owner's manual). This is typically 250-350 kg. You must stay within BOTH the percentage range AND the vehicle's rated maximum.

TIP: Towball weight is affected by how you load the caravan. Heavy items in front of the axle increase it; heavy items behind the axle decrease it. This is the primary lever you have for adjusting towball weight — see Chapter 4.

Where to Find Your Numbers

Every vehicle and caravan sold in Australia must have a compliance plate. Here's where to look:

Tow Vehicle Compliance Plate:

  • Driver's door pillar (most common)
  • Under the bonnet, on the firewall
  • On the passenger door pillar (some models)

It will show: Make, model, VIN, GVM, and sometimes GCM.

Caravan Compliance Plate:

  • A-frame / drawbar (most common)
  • Front offside corner near the coupling
  • Inside a front storage compartment

It will show: Make, model, VIN, ATM, Tare, and sometimes GTM.

[Image: Photo-style illustration of a compliance plate with arrows pointing to each figure]

TIP: Take photos of both compliance plates and save them on your phone. You'll reference these numbers often, and they're much easier to read in a photo than squinting at a metal plate under the chassis in a car park.

Can't find your GCM? Some compliance plates don't show it. Check your vehicle's owner's manual, or search the manufacturer's website for your model's specifications. As a last resort, call the dealer — they can look it up from the VIN.

But notice how tight the vehicle GVM is — only 103 kg to spare. Add a bull bar (35 kg), a roof rack with a storage pod (40 kg), and a long-range fuel tank (30 kg extra fuel), and you've blown the GVM by 2 kg. That's how quickly it adds up.

The Payload Formula at a Glance

Keep these three checks in mind every time you load up:

  1. Vehicle: Loaded vehicle weight ≤ GVM
  2. Caravan: Loaded caravan weight ≤ ATM
  3. Combination: Loaded vehicle + Loaded caravan ≤ GCM

All three must pass. Failing any one means you're overweight.

Chapter 3: Your Vehicle — Know Your Limits

Why "3,500 kg towing capacity" might be the most misleading number in the brochure.

Walk into any ute or SUV dealership in Australia and ask about towing. The salesperson will point to the spec sheet and say: "3,500 kilos braked towing capacity."

It sounds impressive. It sounds like you can tow 3,500 kg. But for most vehicles, under real-world conditions with real passengers and real gear, you can't.

Welcome to the GCM trap.

The GCM Trap — The Calculation Most People Miss

Every tow vehicle has three key ratings:

  1. GVM — Maximum the vehicle can weigh, loaded
  2. Max Braked Towing — Maximum trailer weight it can pull
  3. GCM — Maximum combined weight of vehicle + trailer

Here's what the brochures don't tell you: GVM + Max Towing almost always exceeds GCM.

That means you cannot max out both at the same time. The more you load into the vehicle, the less you can legally tow.

Let's see this in action with real vehicles.

GVM Upgrades — When Your Vehicle Isn't Enough

If your tow vehicle's GVM is too low for your needs, a GVM upgrade may be an option. This involves:

  • What it is: An engineering modification (usually upgraded springs, shocks, and sometimes brakes) that legally increases your vehicle's GVM rating.
  • Who does it: Specialist companies like Lovells, Tough Dog, Pedders, and AEV (Advanced Engine Vehicle). The upgrade must come with an engineering certificate.
  • Typical cost: $2,000–$5,000 depending on the vehicle and upgrade level.
  • Typical increase: 200–400 kg additional GVM.
  • Important: A GVM upgrade increases your vehicle's GVM only. It does NOT change your GCM. If your GCM is the binding constraint (as it often is), a GVM upgrade won't help with towing.

WARNING: A GVM upgrade changes the compliance plate rating. It must be done by a certified installer, and the new rating must be registered with your state's transport authority. A DIY suspension upgrade does NOT count as a GVM increase — the legal GVM remains as stamped on the original compliance plate.

Chapter 4: Your Caravan — Loading It Right

Where you put the weight matters just as much as how much there is.

Your caravan has a payload limit — the difference between its tare weight and its ATM. That number is your budget. Every kilogram of water, food, clothing, gas, tools, and toys comes out of that budget.

But weight compliance isn't just about staying under the limit. It's about how the weight is distributed. A caravan that's within its ATM can still be dangerously unstable if the weight is in the wrong place.

Towball Weight: The Most Misunderstood Number

When your caravan is hitched to your tow vehicle, some of its weight pushes down on the towball. This downward force is the towball weight (also called tow ball mass or TBM).

Getting this number right is critical:

  • Too little towball weight (under 8% of loaded caravan weight): The rear of the caravan is too heavy relative to the front. The caravan becomes tail-happy and prone to dangerous swaying. In extreme cases, the coupling can lift off the ball.

  • Too much towball weight (over 12%): The rear of the tow vehicle is pushed down, lifting the front. This reduces steering responsiveness and front-wheel braking grip. It also adds heavily to the vehicle's GVM.

  • The sweet spot: 8-12% of the loaded caravan weight, with 10% being ideal.

How to Measure Towball Weight

Method 1: Towball scales (most accurate) Purpose-built towball scales sit under the coupling and give a direct reading. Brands like Hayman Reese and Trojan make them. Cost: $150–$300. Worth every cent if you tow regularly.

Method 2: Bathroom scales (budget option) Place a sturdy bathroom scale under the jockey wheel, with the caravan level and unhitched. The reading is your approximate towball weight. This method is less accurate but gives you a ballpark figure.

Method 3: Weighbridge calculation Weigh the vehicle alone, then weigh the vehicle with the caravan hitched. The difference between the two readings is your towball weight. This is accurate but requires a weighbridge visit.

Weight Distribution Fundamentals

[Image: Side-view caravan diagram showing weight distribution zones — front, axle, rear]

The golden rule of caravan loading is simple: heavy items low, centred, and over the axle.

The 60/40 Rule

Aim to place approximately 60% of your cargo weight forward of the axle line, and 40% behind it. This creates the correct towball weight (8-12%) and keeps the caravan stable.

What happens when you get it wrong:

  • Too much weight behind the axle: Towball weight drops, the van becomes tail-heavy, and sway risk increases dramatically. This is the most dangerous loading error and the most common.

  • Too much weight in front of the axle: Towball weight increases excessively, overloading the tow vehicle's rear and potentially exceeding the vehicle's towball rating. The tow vehicle's front lifts, reducing steering and braking grip.

  • Too much weight up high: The caravan's centre of gravity rises, making it more prone to swaying and rollover. This is why heavy items should always go on the floor, never in overhead lockers.

Loading Zones

Think of your caravan in three zones:

Zone 1 — Front (drawbar to axle centre):

  • Batteries and electrical equipment
  • Toolbox and recovery gear
  • Heavy tinned food
  • Generator (if carried)

Zone 2 — Over the axle:

  • Water tanks (most are factory-fitted here for good reason)
  • Gas bottles (in their compartment)
  • Heavy personal items

Zone 3 — Rear (axle centre to rear):

  • Light items only — clothing, bedding, soft goods
  • Outdoor furniture (fold-up chairs are light)
  • Nothing heavy — this zone increases sway risk

WARNING: Never store heavy items in a rear-mounted boot or on a rear bike rack without accounting for the leverage effect. Weight behind the axle acts as a lever that reduces towball weight disproportionately. A 30 kg item stored 2 metres behind the axle has far more effect on stability than a 30 kg item stored directly over the axle.

What to Do About It

  1. Weigh everything. Get a luggage scale ($15 from Kmart) and weigh every item before it goes in the van. Keep a list.

  2. Know your true empty weight. Take the caravan to a weighbridge empty — truly empty, with no water, no gas, no gear. This is your real tare weight. Compare it to the compliance plate. The difference is your aftermarket accessories.

  3. Calculate your real payload. ATM minus your true empty weight = your actual available payload. This is the number that matters.

  4. Travel with half tanks. If you're driving between towns and know you'll have access to water, don't carry full tanks. Water is the heaviest single item in most caravans. Travelling with half tanks can save 50–100 kg instantly.

  5. Audit the "just in case" items. That second generator, the spare set of everything, the tools you've never used — if you haven't needed it in three trips, leave it home.

Chapter 5: The Weighbridge — Your Best Friend

The only way to truly know your weight is to measure it.

You can calculate, estimate, and add up item weights on a spreadsheet all day long. But until you put your rig on a weighbridge, you don't actually know what it weighs.

A weighbridge visit takes 15 minutes and costs $15–$30 (some are free). It's the single most valuable thing you can do for towing safety.

How to Find a Weighbridge

Weighbridges are more common than most people realise. Options include:

  • Public weighbridges — operated by state transport authorities, often found near major highways and freight routes
  • Private weighbridges — at quarries, grain handling facilities, waste transfer stations, and freight depots. Many will weigh the public for a small fee if you ask politely.
  • Council waste transfer stations — many have drive-over scales
  • Caravan-specific weigh services — mobile weighing services that attend caravan shows and camping expos (e.g., VanWeigh, Get-A-Weigh)

To find your nearest weighbridge:

  • Search "public weighbridge [your state]" on your state transport authority website
  • Ask at your local council
  • Check the KamperHub Utilities feature in the app

The Weighbridge Procedure: Step by Step

For a complete weight check, you need three measurements. Here's how to do it:

Measurement 1: Vehicle Only (Loaded As You'd Travel)

Load your tow vehicle exactly as you would for a trip — passengers in their seats, fuel tank full, all gear packed, recovery equipment loaded. Everything except the caravan.

Drive onto the weighbridge. Get weighed.

This number is your actual loaded vehicle weight. Compare it to your GVM. If it exceeds GVM, you're overweight before the caravan is even attached.

Measurement 2: Full Combination (Vehicle + Caravan Coupled)

Now hitch up the fully loaded caravan. Everything in the van — water, gas, food, gear, the lot. As you'd travel.

Drive the full rig onto the weighbridge. Get weighed.

This number is your actual combination weight. Compare it to your GCM. If it exceeds GCM, you're overweight.

Measurement 3: Calculate Towball Weight

If the weighbridge is long enough to weigh the vehicle and caravan separately while coupled, you can get individual axle weights. But most public weighbridges give you a total.

To calculate towball weight from your readings:

Towball weight = (Loaded vehicle weight from Measurement 2)
                 − (Loaded vehicle weight from Measurement 1)

Wait — that doesn't work because Measurement 1 is the vehicle without the caravan. Here's the correct method:

If you can weigh the caravan separately (unhitch and roll it onto the weighbridge on its own jockey wheel):

Caravan standalone weight = ATM check (compare to compliance plate)
Towball weight = Full combination weight − Vehicle alone weight − Caravan standalone weight

Or more simply:

Towball weight = Coupled vehicle weight − Uncoupled vehicle weight

Where "coupled vehicle weight" means the tow vehicle's weight reading while the caravan is hitched (only the vehicle is on the scale, the caravan hangs off the back).

TIP: Many weighbridges have a long enough platform to drive just the vehicle onto it while the caravan trails off the back. This gives you the vehicle weight INCLUDING the towball download. Subtract your uncoupled vehicle weight to get towball weight.

Measurement 4 (Optional): Caravan Only

If possible, unhitch and weigh the caravan alone. This gives you the actual loaded caravan weight to compare directly against ATM.

Not all weighbridges can accommodate an unhitched caravan (they need to be pushed on manually). If you can do it, it's the most direct check.

Understanding Your Weighbridge Ticket

A weighbridge ticket typically shows:

  • Date and time
  • Gross weight (total weight on the platform)
  • Tare weight (if you do a two-visit weigh — empty vehicle first, loaded second)
  • Net weight (gross minus tare)
  • Axle weights (if the bridge has per-axle weighing)

[Image: Sample weighbridge ticket with annotations pointing to each figure]

Keep your weighbridge tickets. They're useful evidence of compliance, and some insurance companies accept them as proof that your rig was within limits.

What to Do If You're Over

Don't panic. Being overweight at the weighbridge is far better than being overweight on the highway. Here's what to do:

Immediate actions:

  1. Identify the heaviest non-essential items and remove them
  2. If water is the issue, dump some water at the weighbridge (many have taps and drains)
  3. Redistribute heavy items from the rear to the front of the caravan
  4. Re-weigh after adjustments to confirm you're compliant

If you can't get under the limit by removing items:

  • Leave non-essential items in storage or with a friend and collect them later
  • Ship heavy items ahead to your destination
  • Consider whether this trip requires all the gear you've packed

Long-term solutions (see Chapter 8 for details):

  • GVM upgrade on the tow vehicle
  • Lighter replacement gear (aluminium instead of steel, lithium instead of AGM)
  • Different caravan with better payload
  • Different tow vehicle with higher ratings

How Often Should You Weigh?

At minimum: Weigh once with a typical trip load. This establishes your baseline.

Ideally: Weigh any time your loading changes significantly — different trip, different number of passengers, new accessories added, or a change to your water/fuel strategy.

After modifications: Any time you add aftermarket accessories to the vehicle or caravan, re-weigh to confirm you're still compliant.

TIP: Many experienced caravanners weigh at the start of each trip season. It takes 15 minutes and gives peace of mind for the entire year.

Chapter 6: State-by-State Regulations

What the law says, state by state.

Weight compliance in Australia is governed by a mix of national and state legislation. For light vehicles (under 4.5 tonnes GVM) towing caravans, the relevant laws are state-based.

Important distinction: The National Heavy Vehicle Law (NHVL) applies only to vehicles with a GVM over 4.5 tonnes. Your tow vehicle and caravan are almost certainly classified as light vehicles, so the NHVL does not apply to you directly. However, the principles are the same — don't exceed your rated weights.

Who is responsible? For private towing with a light vehicle, the driver is solely responsible. There is no "chain of responsibility" for light vehicles. If you borrow someone else's overloaded caravan and get caught, you receive the fine, not the owner.

What Happens at a Roadside Inspection

Police use portable weigh pads — flat electronic scales placed under each wheel. The process takes about 15 minutes and checks:

  1. Driver's licence and registration for both vehicle and caravan
  2. Compliance plates on both (GVM, ATM, GCM)
  3. Actual weights via portable scales
  4. Towball weight
  5. Tyres — condition and suitability for load
  6. Lights, indicators, and trailer connections
  7. Safety chains and coupling condition
  8. Load securing

Possible outcomes:

Outcome When It Applies
All clear — continue your trip Within limits
Infringement notice (fine + demerits) Weight exceeded
Defect notice Unroadworthy item found
Grounding order Cannot continue until load is reduced
Vehicle impoundment Severe safety breach

KEY POINT: A grounding order means you cannot drive the caravan until the weight is brought within limits. On a highway 200 km from home, this means offloading gear by the roadside. It happens.

Licence Requirements

You do not need a special licence to tow a caravan in Australia. A standard Class C (car) licence allows you to:

  • Drive any vehicle with a GVM up to 4.5 tonnes
  • Tow a single trailer (caravan) of any weight, provided you stay within the manufacturer's rated limits (GVM, ATM, GCM)

The limits are set by the vehicle and caravan manufacturers, not by the licence class.

Chapter 8: Solving Overweight Problems

You've been to the weighbridge. You're over. Now what?

Being overweight isn't a permanent sentence. There are solutions at every budget level, from free quick wins to significant investments. The key is to work through them systematically, starting with the easiest and cheapest options.

Tier 1: Quick Wins (Free)

These cost nothing and can save significant weight immediately.

1. Audit the "just in case" pile

Open every cupboard, drawer, and storage compartment in the caravan. Ask yourself: "Did I use this on the last three trips?" If the answer is no, it goes home.

Common culprits:

  • The second set of pots and pans
  • Extra tools beyond the basics
  • Spare parts for things that have never broken
  • Books and magazines (use a Kindle or tablet instead — saves 5-15 kg)
  • "Just in case" camping gear (spare chairs, extra tarps, backup torch)

Many caravanners find 20-50 kg of items they never use.

2. Reduce water

This is the single biggest lever you have.

  • Travelling between towns with reliable water? Don't carry full tanks.
  • Half-fill instead of full-fill. A 200L tank at 50% saves 100 kg.
  • Know where your next water refill point is and plan accordingly.
  • Consider a smaller portable water bladder for remote sections instead of always running full tanks.

3. Audit consumables

  • Food: Don't pack two weeks of food for a five-day trip. Restock at towns along the route.
  • Cleaning supplies: One bottle of each, not backups.
  • Toiletries: Travel-size versions save more than you'd think across a whole family.

4. Redistribute for better balance

Even without removing any weight, redistributing existing items can dramatically improve stability:

  • Move heavy items from rear storage to front/axle area
  • Remove items from high lockers and place them on the floor or in low cabinets
  • Move heavy items from the vehicle to the caravan (or vice versa) to balance GVM compliance

Tier 3: Upgrades (Investment — $500–$5,000)

When quick wins and smart swaps aren't enough, these modifications make a more significant difference.

GVM upgrade on tow vehicle ($2,000–$5,000)

If your vehicle GVM is the binding constraint, a certified GVM upgrade increases the legal maximum loaded weight of your vehicle by 200-400 kg. This involves upgraded springs, shocks, and sometimes brakes, with a new compliance plate and engineering certificate.

Providers include Lovells, Pedders, Tough Dog, and Advanced Engine Vehicle (AEV).

Important: A GVM upgrade increases your vehicle's GVM only. It does NOT change your GCM. If your GCM is the binding constraint (which it often is — see Chapter 3), a GVM upgrade won't help.

Lithium batteries (replacing AGM)

Replacing a single 100Ah AGM battery (28-32 kg) with a 100Ah lithium battery (11-14 kg) saves 15-20 kg. If you have a dual-battery setup, the saving doubles to 30-40 kg.

Cost: $500-$1,500 per battery, but the weight saving is significant and the batteries last 3-4 times longer.

Weight Distribution Hitch ($400–$1,500)

A WDH doesn't reduce weight — it redistributes it. By transferring some of the towball weight back to the vehicle's front axle and the caravan's axle, a WDH improves handling and stability. It can make an otherwise marginal rig feel significantly more planted.

See Chapter 9 for full details.

Suspension upgrades ($500–$2,500)

Upgraded springs or airbag kits on the tow vehicle help it handle load better and maintain ride height. They don't change the legal GVM (unless part of a certified GVM upgrade), but they improve how the vehicle copes with the load.

Chapter 9: Weight Distribution Hitches & Sway Control

Advanced topic, but important for anyone towing a caravan over 2,000 kg.

A Weight Distribution Hitch (WDH) is one of the most effective upgrades for improving towing stability. But it's widely misunderstood — many people think it increases how much you can tow. It doesn't.

What a WDH Does

When you hitch a caravan to your tow vehicle, the towball weight pushes down on the vehicle's rear. This compresses the rear suspension and lifts the front, creating an uneven stance. The front tyres have less grip (reducing steering and braking), and the rear tyres are overloaded.

A WDH uses spring bars (tensioned steel bars that connect the hitch head to the caravan's A-frame) to redistribute the towball weight across all axles — the vehicle's front, the vehicle's rear, and the caravan's axle.

The result:

  • The vehicle sits level instead of tail-down
  • Front tyre grip is restored (better steering and braking)
  • The rear isn't carrying all the towball weight alone
  • The whole rig feels more planted and controlled

[Image: Diagram showing weight distribution — before (sagging rear) and after (level) with spring bar force arrows]

What a WDH Does NOT Do

A WDH does not:

  • Increase your GVM, ATM, or GCM
  • Allow you to tow more than your rated capacity
  • Fix an overloaded caravan
  • Eliminate sway (though it can reduce it)

KEY POINT: A WDH improves how your rig handles the load. It does not increase how much load you can carry. If you're over GCM, a WDH is not the solution.

When You Need One

A WDH is recommended when:

  • Your caravan's loaded weight exceeds 70% of your tow vehicle's kerb weight
  • Your vehicle visibly sags at the rear when the caravan is hitched
  • You notice the steering feels lighter or less responsive when towing
  • High-beam headlights point upward when towing (because the front is lifting)

Most caravan manufacturers and tow vehicle manufacturers recommend a WDH for caravans over 2,000 kg ATM.

Anti-Sway Devices

Sway is the side-to-side oscillation of a caravan. It can be triggered by wind gusts, passing trucks, road imperfections, or driver steering corrections. Mild sway is uncomfortable. Severe sway is terrifying and can lead to loss of control.

Friction sway control:

  • A friction pad that clamps onto the caravan's A-frame
  • Creates resistance to rotation, damping sway
  • Simple and affordable ($100–$300)
  • Requires manual adjustment for different conditions

Dual-cam sway control:

  • Uses cams and brackets that physically resist sway movement
  • More effective than friction alone
  • Often integrated into WDH systems (e.g., Hayman Reese Dual Cam)
  • $300–$600 as an add-on

Electronic Stability Control (ESC):

  • Built into some caravans (e.g., AL-KO ESC)
  • Uses sensors to detect sway and applies the caravan's brakes independently
  • The most effective anti-sway technology available
  • Factory-fitted on many new caravans; can be retrofitted to some

Setup and Adjustment

A WDH must be properly set up to work correctly. Key points:

  1. The vehicle must be level when the WDH is engaged. Measure the vehicle's ride height front and rear, before and after hitching with the WDH engaged. Both measurements should be close to the unladen height.

  2. Spring bar tension must be correct. Too little tension and the WDH does nothing. Too much and the ride is harsh and the front tyres wear unevenly.

  3. The hitch head angle matters. Most WDH systems require a specific angle between the hitch head and the ball mount. Follow the manufacturer's instructions exactly.

  4. Re-adjust when load changes significantly. If you add or remove a lot of weight from the caravan (e.g., filling or emptying water tanks), the WDH tension may need adjustment.

TIP: Have your WDH professionally installed and set up the first time. Many tow bar fitters and caravan service centres offer this service. Watch and learn so you can adjust it yourself for future trips.

Common WDH Mistakes

  1. Not using it at all — many people buy a WDH and leave it in the shed because it's "too much hassle." The 5-minute setup time is worth it.

  2. Over-tightening the spring bars — this transfers too much weight to the front axle, causing excessive front tyre wear and a harsh ride.

  3. Ignoring the WDH on unsealed roads — most WDH manufacturers recommend loosening or removing spring bars on rough unsealed roads to prevent damage to the vehicle and hitch. Check your owner's manual.

  4. Using a WDH to compensate for overloading — a WDH improves handling but doesn't make an overweight rig legal or safe.

Glossary

ATM (Aggregate Trailer Mass): The maximum total weight of a caravan when fully loaded, as rated by the manufacturer. Shown on the caravan's compliance plate.

Ball weight: See Towball weight.

Braked towing capacity: The maximum weight a vehicle can tow when the trailer has its own braking system. Most caravans over 750 kg GTM require brakes.

Compliance plate: A metal plate affixed to a vehicle or caravan by the manufacturer, showing key specifications including weight ratings. It is a legal document.

GCM (Gross Combination Mass): The maximum total weight of the tow vehicle AND trailer combined, as rated by the vehicle manufacturer. Shown on the tow vehicle's compliance plate.

GTM (Gross Trailer Mass): The maximum weight on the caravan's own axles when coupled to the tow vehicle. GTM = ATM minus towball weight.

GVM (Gross Vehicle Mass): The maximum total weight of a vehicle when fully loaded, including passengers, cargo, fuel, and towball weight. Shown on the vehicle's compliance plate.

GVM upgrade: An engineering modification to a vehicle's suspension and/or brakes that legally increases the GVM rating. Must be certified and registered.

Kerb weight: The weight of a vehicle with a full fuel tank and all standard equipment, but no passengers or cargo. Similar to tare weight but typically includes a full fuel tank.

Payload: The weight available for cargo, passengers, and accessories. Calculated as GVM (or ATM) minus tare weight.

Tare weight: The weight of a vehicle or caravan as it left the factory — empty, with standard equipment and minimal fuel (typically 10 litres).

TBM (Tow Ball Mass): See Towball weight.

Towball weight: The downward force the caravan exerts on the tow vehicle's towball when hitched. Should be 8-12% of the loaded caravan weight. Also called ball weight or TBM.

Unbraked towing capacity: The maximum weight a vehicle can tow when the trailer has no brakes. Typically 750 kg in Australia.

WDH (Weight Distribution Hitch): A hitch system that uses spring bars to redistribute towball weight across all axles of the vehicle and caravan, improving stability and levelling the vehicle.
